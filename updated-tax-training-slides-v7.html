VAT - Value Added Tax

How VAT Works

Business charges customer: Product ¬£100 + VAT ¬£20 = ¬£120

Business collects: ¬£20 VAT (Output VAT)

Business pays HMRC: Output VAT minus Input VAT

2025-26 VAT Thresholds

  • Registration threshold: ¬£90,000 (turnover in 12 months)
  • Deregistration threshold: ¬£88,000
  • EU-wide distance selling threshold (post-Brexit): ‚Ç¨10,000
  • UK import threshold for low-value goods: ¬£135 (imports above this are subject to import VAT at point of import)

Distance Selling - UK businesses to EU consumers

Definition

Cross-border online sales from a UK business to consumers in the EU.

Key rule change

  • UK businesses lost the EU distance selling thresholds from 1 January 2021
  • Current EU-wide threshold: ‚Ç¨10,000 for sales to EU consumers

Practical implications

  • If EU consumer sales exceed ‚Ç¨10,000 a year you must either register for VAT in each EU country you sell to or use the EU One Stop Shop (OSS)
  • Below ‚Ç¨10,000 you may charge UK VAT and keep records to show low sales to EU consumers

Red flags for the compliance team

  • High EU consumer sales with no evidence of VAT registration or OSS use
  • Artificially staying just under ‚Ç¨10,000 across reporting periods
  • Inconsistent VAT treatment between customers billed as businesses and consumers

Case example: a small online retailer or app developer with large EU consumer sales but no evidence of VAT compliance. Check platform invoices and export proof.

Digital platforms - App Store and Play Store

How platform sales usually work

  • Platforms commonly act as the merchant of record for consumer purchases and handle VAT collection and remittance in many jurisdictions
  • Developers usually receive net proceeds after platform fees and any VAT the platform has charged
  • Where platforms handle VAT for consumer sales developers often do not need separate VAT registrations for those consumer sales, but check platform invoices

Practical checks for compliance

  • Ask for platform invoices showing VAT charged and remitted
  • If the developer claims complex international VAT burdens, request evidence from the platform
  • When developers sell directly to consumers outside platforms they may have VAT registration obligations in multiple jurisdictions

Common VAT Schemes

Flat Rate Scheme

Small businesses pay a fixed percentage of turnover as VAT, simplifying record-keeping. Input VAT recovery is limited.

Margin Scheme

Used for second-hand goods, works of art and antiques. VAT is charged only on the profit margin, not full selling price.

Tour Operators' Margin Scheme (TOMS)

Applies to travel businesses selling packages. VAT is calculated on the margin between selling price and direct costs of travel services.

UK Businesses Selling in the EU and outside the EU

Goods to the EU

  • UK sellers can zero-rate exports to EU where they hold valid export evidence
  • Import VAT is due in the EU country where the goods arrive and the buyer may pay import VAT at the border
  • UK sellers must check if they need local VAT registration for distance sales above ‚Ç¨10,000 or use OSS

Services to the EU

  • B2B services: place of supply is usually where the customer is established. Supplier normally does not charge UK VAT. Customer accounts for VAT in their country under the reverse charge.
  • B2C services: place of supply is often the supplier's country. Exceptions exist for electronically supplied services and certain professional services.

Sales outside the EU

  • Sales outside the UK and EU are outside the scope of VAT and no VAT is charged.

Place of supply concept

Place of supply decides which country has the right to tax a supply for VAT. It depends on the type of supply and whether the customer is a business or a consumer.

  • For B2B services the place of supply is usually where the customer is established
  • For B2C services the place of supply is usually where the supplier is established unless special rules apply

Case example

Example: A UK web design company sells services to a VAT registered business in Germany. The place of supply is Germany. The UK supplier invoices with no UK VAT. The German business accounts for VAT under the reverse charge on its German VAT return. The UK supplier must keep evidence the customer is VAT registered.

VAT Carousel Fraud (MTIC)

How It Works

  1. EU Supplier: Sells goods VAT-free to UK
  2. Missing Trader: Sells with VAT but disappears
  3. Buffer Company: Claims input VAT refund
  4. Broker: Exports VAT-free back to EU

Red Flags

  • High-value trades in first month
  • Consistent 5-10% margins
  • Same-day trading cycles
  • Mobile phones, chips, carbon credits
  • Large VAT reclaims, minimal substance

üö® Pattern Example

Day 1: Register VAT

Day 30: ¬£500k EU purchase

Day 31: ¬£600k UK sale

Day 32: VAT reclaim

Day 60: Company vanishes

Bogus VAT Refunds

Common Schemes

1. False Export Claims

Claiming goods exported (0% VAT) but sold in UK

  • No export documentation
  • UK residential addresses

2. Inflated Input VAT

Creating fake purchase invoices

  • Suppliers not VAT registered
  • Round figure invoices
  • No delivery evidence

3. Phoenix Trading

Register, claim refund, disappear

  • Immediate large transactions
  • History of dissolved companies
  • Refunds exceed sales

üí° Quick Check

If Input VAT > 20% of purchases, investigate!

Phoenix Trading - Serial Company Formation to Evade Debts

Definition

Phoenix trading is when directors repeatedly create new companies from failed companies, leaving creditors unpaid.

Typical phoenix cycle

  1. Build up debts
  2. Strip assets
  3. Abandon old company
  4. Start new company with same business

Scale

HMRC estimated total losses from phoenixing at around ¬£836 million in 2022-23.

Key red flags

  • Directors with multiple dissolved companies
  • Previous companies with no filed accounts before dissolution
  • Similar business activities across old and new companies
  • Large asset transfers between related companies
  • Rapid company formation after dissolution
  • Similar company names to dissolved entities

Legal vs illegal

Company failure can be legitimate. Deliberate stripping of assets to evade debts is criminal.

HMRC response

HMRC and the Insolvency Service run investigations. Directors can face disqualification and criminal sanctions.

Connection to our work

Watch for these patterns during onboarding and ongoing monitoring to prevent fraud and recover debts.

CIS Red Flags

Businesses Often Missed

  • Property developers
  • Gang masters providing labour
  • Scaffolders, demolition
  • Painting, decorating

Common Evasion Methods

  • False status: Employees as subcontractors
  • Non-registration: Avoiding CIS completely
  • Cash payments: No deductions made
  • Invoice splitting: Stay below thresholds

Key points for compliance

  • Zero-rated vs exempt: Zero-rated supplies are charged at 0 percent and input VAT can usually be reclaimed. Exempt supplies are outside VAT and input VAT cannot be reclaimed.
  • Examples: Many basic food items and young children‚Äôs clothing are zero-rated. Financial services, education and some health services are commonly exempt.
  • Selling to EU customers: For goods exported to the EU, UK sellers normally treat the sale as an export and may zero-rate VAT where valid export evidence exists. For sales to EU consumers monitor the ‚Ç¨10,000 threshold and register in relevant EU states or use the OSS if required. For services the VAT treatment depends on place of supply rules and whether the customer is a business or consumer.
  • Reverse charge: For many cross-border B2B supplies the buyer accounts for VAT under the reverse charge. This shifts reporting from the supplier to the buyer and is an anti-fraud measure.
  • Practical red flags: High EU sales with no VAT evidence, inconsistent VAT on invoices, frequent 'exports' with weak shipping paperwork, large imports close to ¬£135, and claims of complex platform handling without matching platform invoices.

Takeaways from the Tax Training Session

  • You now have a basic understanding of why some users are VAT registered and others are not.
  • Many EU-related sales show 0% VAT because they fall under reverse charge rules.
  • Not all differences in VAT treatment signal wrongdoing.

When reviewing cases:

  • Tax evasion is rarely clear cut unless it is very obvious.
  • The key factor is intent to evade ‚Äì and intent is difficult to prove.
  • If intent is not obvious, do not escalate immediately.
  • Instead, ask questions first and gather more information.

Practical steps:

  • Confirm whether the user has an accountant.
  • If they do, request accountant correspondence.
  • Where a professional firm is engaged, tax compliance becomes their responsibility, not ours.

When to escalate:

  • Only pursue further if there are other serious concerns, such as:
    • Signs of money laundering
    • Questionable legitimacy of the trade

Key message:

Focus on asking the right questions.
If tax intent is unclear, it is not automatically a financial crime case.
Your role is to flag risks, not prove tax evasion.
